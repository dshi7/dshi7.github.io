## Synchronization

When hauling data via `cp.async` or TMA, threads don’t stall. They proceed unhindered, overlapping latency by prepping the next iteration while the hardware operates concurrently.

Yet, this structural autonomy demands a deterministic contract. Imagine a standard SMEM ring-buffer pipeline (`buf[0:2]`):

- **TMA Engine:** Continuously floods `buf[0]` through `buf[2]` with GMEM data.
- **MMA Engine:** Sequentially drains those same coordinates for Tensor Core computation.

The missing substrate here is orchestration. We need a mechanism equivalent to three sentinels monitoring `buf[0:2]` to shout the exact moment a buffer is full, and another three to signal when the MMA engine has entirely evacuated the data (empty), clearing it safely for reuse.

Those 'sentinels' are mbarriers. Just denote them bar\_full and bar\_empty here. They shout when their built-in phases are flipped.

- **bar\_full** is intrinsically tethered to the TMA engine and knows how many bytes to expect. The moment the TMA hardware finishes pouring that data packet into SMEM, it automatically triggers the phase flip, unblocking the consumer.
- **bar\_empty** is bound with MMA and works in a classical software semaphore behavior. It relies on explicit warp-level arrival tracking and requires a manual arrival command to flip phase.

This is the most fundamental, DSL-agnostic picture of how mbarriers work. Refer to other resources (mainly PTX documentations) about finer details: syntax, named barrier vs mbarrier (though sync story is a bit different), remote barriers, etc. With the correct mental model, those nuances are easy to learn and many advanced kernels are easy to understand.

This comprises the foundational, DSL-agnostic paradigm of asynchronous memory barriers. Finer syntactic nuances, structural resources (named bars vs mbars), and spatial extensions like remote cluster sync can be readily parsed from the PTX manual. Once this core mental model is crystalized, navigating the architectural nuances of SOTA kernels becomes a piece of cake.
